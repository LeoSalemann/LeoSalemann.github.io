Planning an Approach

First thing I gotta do is make a "gauge" with different slot thicknesses. My calipers give me a thickness of 0.1580 in. Adobe Illustrator seems to stop at 0.01 inch increments. Okay, we'll make slots at 0.16, 0.17, 0.18 0.2.

Drawing the Gauge

I was hoping I could draw a rectangle, add some points, and cut things out. Adobe Illustrator doesn't seem to let me do that (not without a fight, anyway), so I'll go line by line instead. Not has hard as I thought, once you start copy/pasting the repetitive lines.

Well, crap. Even my tightest slot is too loose. Need to figure out how to see sub-tenth line lenghts. Aha! clicking Shape gets you a form with exactl lenth and angle. Going to change up my slots to be 0.146 0.148 0.150 0.152 0.154.

Aha! Adobe Illustrator provides a Shape toolbar item that gives you full control over line length!

Success! ...just barely. The tighest slots (0.146 in) fit great. Tempted to make that my new midpoint and try for even tighter, but it's getting late and I need my beauty sleep.

Building the Actual Kit

Okay, it's been a couple days since the gauge build. Imma build a frame for a wing. Grab a legit NACA Airfoil, cut some notches in it, replicate, and include some more parts for the wing outline & a spart or two. I need 12 parts, so ... a wing outline, a spar, ten airofils. Maybe a few more spars and less foils.

I've had another epiphany! I shouldn't need to do a bunch of line-by-line tedium like I did for the guage. A simple overlapping rectangle should do just fine! Add a triangle, and I can get a campher!. Don't know what I'm talking about? Here's a sketch:

Okay so let's try this. I'm gonna find a symmetrical airfiol, and make a little "3D teardrop" like this:

First-off, let's find an arfoil on arfoiltools.com, download as SVG, then import into Adobe Illutrator. This one looks promising.

The SVG loaded into Adobe Illustrator okay, but it inlcuded all the gridlines and titles and junk with it. After learning a bit more mouseology, I could isolate the airfoil. Add soome rectangles for slots, triangle for camphers, and we're ready to print.

Not bad. Used a lower power setting so some of the cuts didn't go all the way through. Had to do a bit of touch-up with an Exacto, but they came out.

Pretty happy with how things assembled. Nice flush fit with between the circle and the two airofils. Need to make the parts larger next time, and aovid long thing tapers. Also keep up with those triangle camphers.

Scaling Up

Right, then; our little "drop tank" test proves this will work, let's make a wing! Back to the sketch book. We'll want a "rectangular hoop" for the basic wing frame. Then a bunch of airfoil ribs, and three spars. So to meet requirements, we need at least eight ribs for a total of 12 parts.

Well, that was a headache. LOTS of little elements to line up. Had to draw a bunch of guidelines. Which I ALMOST FORGOT to delete before printing.

Headache #2. I had my airfoil set up as a library element, so I lost it when I copied my ai doc over to thumbrdive to print from one of the UW MILL machines. Now let's try printing ....

Uh-Oh

I've learned a valuable lesson about print order. Pretty terifying to watch some parts "scallop up" before the print's done. Everything came out okay, though.

Headache #3. I discovered I forgot some of the rectangular cutouts. Had to improvise with a utility knife, but built myself a mini wing.

Okay, time to set up the layers for the main Big Wing.

Putting all the layers together, you can see how the guidelines help to keep all recuagular cutouts lined up. You can also see how it would utterly destroy the ouptut if we actually printed them.

Okay, Let's Start Printing(Finally)!

Printing by layers was pretty easy. Just selectively turn one layer on and all the others off, and make sure "Print Visible Layers" is enabled.

Crap. A lot of the small parts didn't cut all the way through. Forgot to set my power at 100. Time for the exacto knife again.

Finished Product!

Not bad. Pretty happy with the way the wing spars meshed into the airfoils. Too bad the front edge of the frame juts beyond the arfoils. Looks like I didn't have the airofil & cutouts properly aligned. Good enough, though for a proof of concept.
